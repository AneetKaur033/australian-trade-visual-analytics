# Australian International Trade — Visual Analytics

A visual analytics project exploring 35+ years of Australian international trade data (1988–2024), built in Tableau with statistical preparation in Excel. 

---

## Project Overview

This project analyses Australia's international trade patterns using data from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS), covering 10 main categories and 67 subcategories across both imports and exports. The goal was to communicate complex trade trends clearly for a government and policy audience.

The analysis moves from a broad overview of all main categories down to a focused deep-dive on Mineral Fuels - specifically coal and petroleum - including a 10-year trend analysis and 5-year forecast.

---

## What I Built

**Excel preparation:**
- Calculated year-on-year percentage changes for all categories and subcategories from 1989 onward
- Computed each category's share of total annual trade value (statistical patterns)
- Combined import and export data into unified sheets with a Trade label column for Tableau

**Tableau visualisations:**
- Line charts tracking raw import and export dollar values across all 10 main categories (1988–2024)
- Area charts showing each category's share of total trade over time
- Bar and stacked bar charts comparing subcategory breakdowns (Food & Live Animals focus)
- Combo chart comparing coal exports vs petroleum imports on dual axes
- Dashboard focused on Mineral Fuels — Coal and Petroleum subcategories
- 6-scene storyboard walking through the full narrative from main categories to subcategory insights
- 10-year trend analysis and 5-year forecast (2025–2029) for petroleum imports

---

## Key Findings

- Machinery and transport equipment dominated imports, growing from ~$17B in 1988 to ~$180B by 2024
- Mineral fuels and crude materials together make up nearly 60% of Australia's exports by 2024, up from a much smaller share in 1988
- Coal is Australia's largest mineral fuel export, peaking at $142B, but faces long-term risk from energy transition pressures
- Australia exports large volumes of coal and gas but imports ~99% of its petroleum needs due to limited domestic refining capacity
- Petroleum imports peaked above $65B in 2022 following a sharp COVID-related drop in 2020
- Food exports grew from under $10B in 1988 to $52.7B in 2024, led by meat (40%) and cereals (26%)
